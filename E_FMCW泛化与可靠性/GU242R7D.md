# Eficient FMCW Radar Interference Suppression via Adaptive Learning Framework With Localized Sparse Prior

Hao Zhang , Shunjun Wei , Senior Member, IEEE, Yinghao Cheng, Graduate Student Member, IEEE, Shuangqiao Li, Student Member, IEEE, Rui Min , Member, IEEE, Jin Li , Member, IEEE, Mou Wang , Member, IEEE, and Guolong Cui , Senior Member, IEEE

Abstract—Frequency-modulated continuous-wave (FMCW) radar systems sufer from severe mutual interference in dense low-altitude uncrewed aerial vehicle (UAV) environments, critically degrading target detection fidelity and spectral eficiency in shared microwave bands. To address the challenges above, this article proposes an adaptive perceptual learning target/interference network, named APLF-Net. Initially, a multivariate coupled perception model is proposed to co-optimize the objective function with auxiliary sparse priors. Subsequently, a stepwise alternating optimization framework is designed and integrated into a hierarchical deep feedforward network for enhancing flexibility. Within this framework, the hierarchical network structure is refined to mimic optimization steps, accelerating the model data processing. The threshold convolution module based on echo energy inference is proposed to prevent excessive sparsification. Furthermore, a novel unsupervised training strategy is introduced, which leverages the intrinsic connections within the signal model to invert the sparse representations output by the network back into the original input echo, thereby completing the loss term mapping. A series of simulations and real-world experiments demonstrates that, compared to existing state-of-the-art methods, APLF-Net can rapidly suppress interference and accurately extract targets, exhibiting good performance.

Index Terms—Frequency-modulated continuous-wave (FMCW) radar, hierarchical network, interference suppression, unsupervised training.

## A. Related Works

In recent years, suppressing mutual interference among FMCW radar systems has emerged as a key area of focus for researchers globally. To tackle this issue, numerous solutions have been developed. This study categorizes these solutions into two distinct groups: interference suppression based on radar hardware and system-level strategies, and those that are based on signal processing.

Hardware-based and strategy-driven interference suppression methods efectively reduce the risk of mutual interference between radar systems by optimizing radar antenna designs, system layout, and hardware configurations. In terms of antenna design, some studies have employed adaptive beamforming techniques to dynamically control the beam direction, thereby avoiding signal overlap [8], [9]. In addition, adjusting the polarization of the receiving antenna can efectively mitigate interference caused by polarization mismatch [10], [11]. Regarding radar system layout, the design of a centralized control platform to assign dedicated parameters to each radar unit can prevent interference at its source [12]. Moreover, Hakobyan et al. [13] proposed a cognitive radar system capable of dynamically sensing environmental interference and adjusting radar parameters in real time to actively suppress the interference. However, this centralized control and resource allocation approach demands high coordination and frequent data exchange, which adds to system complexity. On the hardware front, modulating and coding radar waveforms can enhance system robustness and improve signal-to-noise ratio [14], [15], [16]. Nevertheless, hardware- and strategy-based interference suppression methods often lead to significant increases in system complexity, resulting in higher design, manufacturing, and maintenance costs, which adversely afect radar system integration and scalability [17].

Signal processing-based interference suppression methods mainly involve analyzing and processing the received signals under existing radar hardware conditions. This article categorizes these methods into five types: time-domain methods, frequency-domain methods, filter-based methods, deep learning methods, and compressed sensing (CS) methods. First, time-domain interference suppression techniques are advantageous because interference signals typically exhibit high-amplitude, transient characteristics in the time series [18], which provides a feasible approach for time-domain interference removal, efectively validated in previous studies [19], [20]. For frequency-domain methods, the primary approach is to analyze the frequency spectrum of the echo signals and remove the interference components while preserving the target signal. Empirical mode decomposition (EMD) can decompose the echo signal into multiple frequency components [21], [22], [23], enabling selective removal of specific interference frequencies. In addition, the short-time Fourier transform (STFT) can analyze the frequency distribution of both target and interference signals across diferent time intervals, thereby efectively removing interference or extracting target signals [24], [25]. Moreover, constant false alarm rate (CFAR) techniques can be applied for locating and filtering interference signals in the time–frequency domain [26], [27]. Besides, the fractional Fourier transform has also been applied to interference suppression and has achieved favorable results [28]. However, time-domain and frequency-domain methods face the challenge that when target and interference signals overlap in the spectrum, suppressing the interference may also inadvertently remove parts of the target signal, leading to a loss of valuable information.

The principle of filter-based methods involves selectively retaining the components of the target signal while efectively suppressing the interference components [29], [30], [31]. For instance, Lee et al. [32] proposed a time-domain low-pass filter to extract interference signals, which were then subtracted from the original echo to recover the target signal. Furthermore, Rohman et al. [33] designed an adaptive filter based on the least-mean-square (LMS) algorithm and the Hilbert transform, which efectively mitigated interference and emphasized the target signal. Besides, singular value decomposition (SVD) has also been demonstrated to efectively distinguish between interference and target signals [34]. However, filterbased suppression methods also have certain limitations: they may cause attenuation of the target signal’s energy, and when the interference signal characteristics change or do not match the preset filter parameters, the filtering performance may degrade, afecting the quality of target signal recovery.

With the rapid development of artificial intelligence, deep learning techniques have gradually been applied to radar interference suppression [35]. Some studies leverage the powerful feature extraction capabilities of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to extract target features across multiple domains from complex signals or to learn and remove interference signal features [36], [37], [38], [39], [40]. However, there is a lack of open-source real interference datasets, and the simulated interference data cannot accurately reflect the clutter distribution and noise in real-world scenarios. Furthermore, the quality of labeled data greatly limits the efectiveness and generalizability of supervised networks.

In recent years, CS has demonstrated significant potential in interference suppression [41]. Chen et al. [42] employed sparse Bayesian inference to identify and filter out interference and noise components from the target signal. In addition, several works [43], [44] have introduced the sparse-augmented Lagrangian shrinkage algorithm (SALSA) to separate the sparse representations of target and interference signals in diferent domains [45]. Furthermore, Xu [46] proposed a model optimization-based target extraction algorithm using the dual $l _ { 1 }$ norm, which achieved promising results. What is more, leveraging the low-rank and sparse characteristics of the signal [47] can efectively suppress interference and extract targets. However, the methods mentioned above primarily focus on single-chirp interference suppression, where a set of optimal hyperparameters cannot be universally applied to all chirps, leading to weakened interchirp relationships and consequently degrading the quality of the RD map. In our previous work, we innovatively combined deep learning with CS to adaptively learn the hyperparameters for each chirp [48], but it incurs duplicated computational overhead when processing a frame of data. For multichirp signals, sparse priors in both the time and frequency domains have been shown to separate target and interference signals efectively [49]. In addition, tensor decomposition techniques have also been employed to remove interference in multichirp signals [23]. Overall, CS-based methods excel in target extraction, but their heavy reliance on intricate parameter tuning and hyperparameter selection limits real-time applicability in engineering scenarios.

We have proposed a deep neural network for feature extraction of the target signal in the RD domain [50]. However, this method does not account for the feature representation of interference signals, which may lead to the loss of discriminative features during iterative processing. Inspired by the challenges discussed above, we extend the aforementioned approach by proposing a novel perceptual learning target/interference network, named APLF-Net, which leverages auxiliary sparse prior of the target/interference signals to suppress the interference from multichirp data synergistically. First, a joint target–interference-coupled optimization model is developed. Subsequently, a stepwise alternating optimization framework is designed and integrated into a learnable hierarchical deep feedforward network. In addition, a threshold convolution module based on echo energy inference is introduced to prevent excessive sparsification. Last, a novel unsupervised training strategy is proposed, which utilizes the intrinsic relationships within the signal model to invert the original echoes, thereby achieving the mapping of loss terms. Extensive simulations and real-world experiments validate the efectiveness of APLF-Net. Overall, the innovations and contributions of the proposed method can be summarized as follows.

## III. PROPOSED METHOD

This section proposes a novel perception learning framework, termed APLF-Net, for efective suppression of the mutual interference analyzed in Section II. Leveraging the sparse characteristics of both targets and interference across multiple chirps, we first construct a localized optimization model. Building on this, a localized feature perception framework (LFPF) enables joint estimation of targets and interference through simultaneous updates. Finally, the framework is integrated into an unfolded feedforward neural network architecture, where parameters are adaptively tuned to achieve optimal interference suppression.

Consequently, Y can be decomposed into $\{ \mathbf { y } _ { 1 } , \mathbf { y } _ { 2 } , \ldots , \mathbf { y } _ { N _ { p } } \}$ consisting of $N _ { p }$ chirp vectors, $\mathbf { y } _ { i } ~ \in ~ \mathbb { C } ^ { N _ { r } \times 1 }$ <sup>, ,</sup> <sup>.</sup> <sup>.</sup> <sup>.</sup> <sup>,</sup>. As shown in Fig. 2, interference signals generate instantaneous high-power energy peaks in both 1-D and 2-D time domains. Existing studies [45], [46], [49] have confirmed the sparsity of interference signals in the time domain. Besides, radar targets exhibit point sparsity in the 1-D range dimension [43], [45], [46], [48], [53], and line sparsity in the slow-time and range dimensions [49]. However, when the power of interference signals is excessively high, it can obscure weak targets in the range dimension, making it dificult to separate them from the interference signals. Since Y includes multiple chirps, incorporating target speed information can enhance prior knowledge, thereby achieving more precise target detection. Therefore, unlike the aforementioned studies, we have optimized the measurement matrix for target signals, enabling simultaneous compression of target signals in both the fast-time and slow-time dimensions, as shown below

$$
\mathbf { Y } = \mathbf { A } _ { 1 } \mathbf { X } \mathbf { A } _ { 2 } + \mathbf { I } + \mathbf { N }\tag{7}
$$

Analogous to conventional CS-based methodologies, LFPF encounters challenges in the selection of hyperparameters. Due to the potential complex interdependencies among hyperparameters, the parameter space becomes high-dimensional and intricate, rendering the optimization of optimal hyperparameters highly challenging. Even when successfully optimized, the process demands substantial computational resources and time, making it impractical for real-world engineering applications. To address these challenges, we integrate the LFPF with deep learning techniques to achieve eficient and high-performance interference suppression.

## B. APLF-Net

To achieve precise separation of X and I, this article aims to embed the LFPF into a deep unfolding network, namely APLF-Net, and optimize the internal hyperparameters by training to minimize the loss function, thus improving the performance of the model.

1) Network Structure: The structure of APLF-Net is shown in Fig. 3. The network consists of T layers of LFPF, with each layer representing one iteration of LFPF based on (18). Here, Y denotes the input echo signal with interference, and $\mathbf { X } ^ { T }$ represents the final estimated signal in the RD domain. In addition, OTPM refers to the optimal threshold approximation module. The purple section illustrates the inversion path of the original echo, while the red dashed lines indicate the connection to the loss function. The above components will be explained in detail in the following.

At each layer of the APLF-Net, tensors X, I, Z, and U are optimized according to (8), with the updated tensors being passed to the next layer, guiding the model toward the global optimum. To reduce computational complexity, we replace $\mathbf { A } _ { 1 } , \mathbf { A } _ { 2 } , \mathbf { A } _ { 1 } ^ { H }$ , and ${ \bf A } _ { 1 } ^ { H }$ with the fast Fourier transform (FFT) and inverse FFT (IFFT) applied directly along the rows and columns of the tensor. The updates of X and I at the tth layer can then be written as follows:

$$
\mathbf { I } ^ { t } = \frac { 1 } { \rho + 1 } \ \left( \mathbf { Y } - \tilde { \mathcal { F } } _ { r } \left( \tilde { \mathcal { F } } _ { c } \left( \mathbf { X } ^ { t - 1 } \right) \right) + \rho \left( \mathbf { Z } ^ { t - 1 } - \mathbf { U } ^ { t - 1 } \right) \right)\tag{19}
$$

$$
\mathbf { X } ^ { t } = \delta \left( \frac { \mathcal { F } _ { r } \left( \mathcal { F } _ { c } \left( \mathbf { I } ^ { t } - \mathbf { Y } + \mathbf { U } ^ { t - 1 } \right) \right) } { \rho } , \frac { \lambda _ { 2 } + \alpha \lambda _ { c } } { \rho } \right)\tag{20}
$$

where $\mathcal { F } _ { r } ( \cdot )$ and $\mathcal { F } _ { c } ( \cdot )$ represent the FFT applied along the rows and columns, respectively. Similarly, $\tilde { \mathcal { F } } _ { r } ( \cdot )$ and $\tilde { \mathcal { F } } _ { c } ( \cdot )$ correspond to the IFFT applied along the rows and columns, respectively. Besides, denotes a weighting factor. To avoid excessive signal loss or over-sparsification, we have designed OTPM, which calibrates <sub>2</sub> in (20) by extracting the magnitude of Y in time domian $( | \mathbf { Y } | \in \mathbb { R } ^ { N _ { r } \times N _ { p } } )$ , thereby enabling more precise control over the sparsity of the target signal. The specific structure of OTPM is shown in Fig. 4, where the input is the magnitude of Y and the output is the corrected feature value $\lambda _ { c } \in \mathbb { R }$ . In addition, GAP refers to global average pooling. The data processing flow of OTPM can be expressed as below

$$
\lambda _ { c } = \theta _ { g a p } \left( \theta _ { s } \left( \theta _ { c } \left( \theta _ { r } \left( \theta _ { c } \left( \left| \mathbf { Y } \right| , \mathbf { w } _ { 1 } \right) \right) , \mathbf { w } _ { 2 } \right) \right) \right) .\tag{21}

2) Training Strategy: Currently, radar environments in real-world applications are typically complex and dynamic, making it challenging to obtain perfectly corresponding end-to-end interference and noninterference data in actual scenarios. Although simulated data containing both interference and noninterference can be generated for supervised network training, the presence of ground noise and clutter often prevents these simulated datasets from fully capturing the characteristics of real environments. In addition, supervised learning methods heavily rely on large amounts of manually labeled data, and when data is insuficient or does not cover all application scenarios, the model may struggle to efectively handle variations in interference signal intensity and noise levels. To address these challenges, this article proposes an unsupervised learning approach for training APLF-Net.

In previous work [48], learnable matrices were used to capture the interference signal features, which were then combined with the target signal to establish a mapping between the input echoes and the target signals. However, this approach typically involves a large number of parameters, which significantly increases the computational overhead during training and inference, as well as potentially causing instability. Therefore, considering the relationship between the outputs $\mathbf { X } ^ { T }$ and $\mathbf { I } ^ { T }$ of the APLF-Net and the input echo Y, as illustrated by the purple solid lines in Fig. 3, we utilize $\mathbf { X } ^ { T }$ and $\mathbf { I } ^ { T }$ to obtain the inverted echo $\bar { \bf Y }$ containing both the interference and target signals, as described below

$$
\bar { \mathbf { Y } } = \tilde { \mathcal { F } } _ { r } \left( \tilde { \mathcal { F } } _ { c } \left( \mathbf { X } ^ { T } \right) \right) + \mathbf { I } ^ { T } .\tag{22}
$$

This inversion process reconstructs the input echo by combining the output of APLF-Net in the time domain. By minimizing the diference between this reconstructed echo and the original input, the network learns to perform efective signal separation without requiring clean data for supervision, forming the core of the unsupervised training strategy.

## V. CONCLUSION

This article proposes an eficient perception-based interference and target learning network, named APLF-Net. The method combines the theoretical framework of CS with the feature extraction advantages of deep learning, thereby adaptively suppressing interference in FMCW radar signals under the guidance of sparse priors. First, a coupled model for joint optimization of targets and interference based on multichirp data is established. Next, a stepwise alternating optimization framework is designed and integrated into a deep network with hierarchical learning. The hierarchical structure simulates the optimization steps, efectively utilizing critical information from both targets and interference to assist the optimization process. To prevent excessive sparsification during learning, a threshold convolution module based on echo energy inference is proposed, guiding the network to approximate the optimal parameters. To overcome the limitations of simulated interference data on network training, a novel unsupervised training strategy is introduced. This strategy utilizes the intrinsic relationships in the signal model to jointly invert the interference and target signals output by the network, bringing them closer to the original echo, thus better learning data distribution. Finally, a series of simulations and measured experiments are conducted to validate the efectiveness of the proposed method, compared with state-of-the-art methods. The experimental results demonstrate that APLF-Net achieves a significant SSIM of 0.9912 and reduces RMSE to 0.0517 under challenging −15-dB SINR conditions, while attaining a target detection probability of 97.52% with an average processing time of only 0.0807 s/frame. The qualitative and quantitative results confirm that APLF-Net can accurately extract targets while efectively suppressing interference in various experimental scenarios.

Although the proposed method achieves a certain level of generalization capability through unsupervised training without labeled data, it still faces limitations when encountering new experimental scenarios with significantly diferent interference or clutter characteristics. If the pretrained model fails to adapt efectively to such unseen conditions, retraining would be required. Therefore, in future work, we will focus on developing accelerated training strategies to enhance the model’s adaptability and enable rapid deployment in diverse real-world environments.
