Abstract—In this paper we propose a novel method for the mitigation of uncorrelated frequency-modulated continuous-wave (FMCW) automotive radar interferences based on the shorttime Fourier transform (STFT) and L-statistics. The idea of the method is to compute the STFT of the beat signal and sort each constant frequency line of the spectrogram in ascending order, which places the bins affected by interference towards the right hand side of the time-frequency plane. Onwards, the interferencefree range profile is computed by a coherent summation along the time axis of the bins considered to be unaffected by interference (the first q% of time bins from the sorted spectrogram). The impact of the STFT parameters (sliding window length and step size) and the summation percentage q on the method’s performances and computation time is investigated using a synthetic dataset that contains 24000 samples of realistic automotive radar signals (with multiple targets and interference sources). It is shown that the developed method can mitigate multiple interference sources. Moreover, by increasing the sliding window step size to half the window’s length we obtain similar performances to the case with unitary step size (the classical STFT definition) and the computation time decreases significantly, which makes the method more suitable for real time implementation on a digital signal processor. The method is compared with state-of-the-art interference mitigation approaches on the synthetic dataset and real data acquired with a W-band automotive radar.

Index Terms—automotive radar, interference mitigation, Short-Time Fourier Transform, L-statistics

## I. CONTEXT AND STATE OF THE ART

The radar systems used in the automotive industry are in general frequency-modulated continuous-wave (FMCW) radars, which transmit sequences of linear chirp signals with a high duty cycle. The increasing number of autonomous vehicles [1] involves a big density of automotive radars on the streets, and taking into account that these systems operate in a limited bandwidth (76-77 GHz for long range and 77- 81 GHz for short-range applications), a higher probability of mutual interference between radar sensors will be present in a heavy traffic situation. Depending on the difference between the time-frequency slopes of the transmitted and interfering chirp signals, the mutual interference can create either a ghost target in the range profile when the chirps have the same slopes (correlated interference) or can increase the effective noise floor and reduce sensitivity when the slopes are different (uncorrelated interference) [2]. In recent years, a lot of detection and mitigation radio frequency interference (RFI) mitigation methods have been developed (e.g. [2], [3], [4], [5], [6], [7], [8]).

To mitigate the effect of mutual interferences one can either use methods that involve detection and suppression of the interference, or methods that directly supress the interference without a prior detection step. The simplest state-of-the-art method to mitigate interferences is to detect in the beat signal the samples affected by interference and set to zero the values that exceed a certain threshold (,,zeroing” technique). This method can have a big disavantage if the duration of the interference extends over most of the chirp duration or in the presence of multiple inteferences because the number of removed samples can be quite high and we end up loosing most of the useful samples. To solve this problem, in [9] and [10] were presented methods that replace the affected samples with other values obtained from neighbouring signal without interference. Another time domain method presented in [11], proposes a decomposition of the received signal (into useful beat signal and interference) using morphological component analysis (MCA).

The method proposed in this paper is based on the shorttime Fourier transform (STFT) and L-statistics. The STFT is computed for every beat signal, and each constant frequency line of the spectrogram is sorted in ascending order, which places the bins affected by interference towards the right hand side of the time-frequency plane. Onwards, the interferencefree range profile is computed by a coherent summation along the time axis of the bins considered to be unaffected by interference (a certain percent of the first time bins from the sorted spectrogram). Compared to most state-of-the-art procedures for RFI mitigation, this method does not involve an interference detection step (e.g., the STFT-based RFI mitigation method presented in [12] requires a detection step, whereas in our approach the same processing flow can be employed with/without interference) and can mitigate multiple sources of interference (all the time-frequency bins affected by interference are implictly placed on the right hand side of the spectrogram and are not included in the final summation). Moreover, by exploiting the properties of the STFT, the computation time can be significantly reduced (with a slight performance degradation) by using a step size of the sliding window equal to half the window’s length.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. In Section II we introduce the model of the beat signal affected by uncorrelated interference and present the developed interference mitigation method. After that, in Section III we perform a parametric analysis of the method’s performances using a synthetic dataset of automotive radar signals. Section IV presents a comparison of the proposed method with state of the art using the synthetic dataset and real W-band signals affected by interference. Finally, Section V concludes the paper.

## II. METHODOLOGY

This section presents the FMCW radar signal model in the presence of interferences, a few computation aspects related to the STFT and the proposed RFI mitigation method.

## A. Radar signal model

The signal transmitted by an FMCW radar $s _ { T } ( t )$ in one repetition interval is a chirp signal with linear frequency variation:

$$
s _ { T } ( t ) = \cos \left[ 2 \pi \left( f _ { c } t + \frac { 1 } { 2 } k ( t - T / 2 ) ^ { 2 } \right) \right] p \left( \frac { t - \frac { T } { 2 } } { T } \right)\tag{1}
$$

where

$$
p \left( { \frac { t } { T } } \right) = { \left\{ \begin{array} { l l } { 1 , } & { { \mathrm { i f } } \ | t | \leq { \frac { T } { 2 } } } \\ { 0 , } & { { \mathrm { i f } } \ | t | > { \frac { T } { 2 } } } \end{array} \right. }\tag{2}
$$

is the gate function of duration $T$ (corresponding to one repetition interval), $f _ { c }$ is the carrier frequency and k represents the slope of the transmitted chirp signal (the ratio between the chirp’s bandwidth B and its duration T ). The transmitted signal is backscattered from the target and received after a time $\tau _ { i }$ that is related to the radar-target distance $R _ { i } = { c \tau _ { i } } / 2$ (where c is the speed of light in air). The received signal in the presence of mutual interferences is a sum between the signals reflected by the targets and the interference chirp signals:

$$
s _ { R } ( t ) = \sum _ { i = 1 } ^ { N _ { T } } A _ { R , i } s _ { T } ( t - \tau _ { i } ) + \sum _ { l = 1 } ^ { N _ { R F I } } s _ { R F I , l } ( t ) ,\tag{3}
$$

where $N _ { T }$ is the number of targets, $N _ { R F I }$ is the number of interference sources, and $A _ { R , i }$ is the amplitude of target i. The received signal is multiplied with the transmitted signal and low-pass filtered with an anti-aliasing filter having the cut-off frequency $F _ { M }$ . Onwards we consider that the downconversion is performed using a quadrature mixer (complex-baseband architecture). After mixing the signal reflected by a pointlike target with the transmitted signal we obtain a constant frequency signal (the classical beat signal), whereas by mixing an un-correlated interference with the transmitted chirp we obtain a baseband chirp signal (as depicted in a qualitative manner in Fig. 1).

![](images/94c5aad8a5129cbd06a938b33ce00710a0605d0ee88666e593947231c8a9aba7.jpg)  
Fig. 1: Qualitative time-frequency diagrams of (a) the transmitted signal and an un-correlated interference and (b) the resulting baseband interference signal after mixing with the transmitted signal.

The slope of the interference baseband chirp is equal to the difference between the slope of the transmitted signal k and the slope of the interference $k _ { R F I , l }$ , while its zerofrequency point ${ ^ { \circ } } _ { \cdot R F I , l }$ corresponds to the intersection between the instantaneous frequency laws (IFL) of the transmitted and interference chirps. Based on the time-frequency diagram from Fig. 1b, the IFL of the interference baseband chirp can be expressed as:

$$
\begin{array} { l } { f _ { R F I , l } ( t ) = ( k - k _ { R F I , l } ) ( t - t _ { R F I , l } ) \times } \\ { \times p \left( \frac { t - { t _ { R F I , l } } } { T _ { A A F , l } } \right) p \left( \frac { t - \frac { T } { 2 } } { T } \right) , } \end{array}\tag{4}
$$

where

$$
T _ { A A F , l } = { \frac { 2 F _ { M } } { | k - k _ { R F I , l } | } }\tag{5}
$$

is the duration of the interference (limited by the anti-aliasing filter used before sampling). Note that if the slope of the transmitted signal is close to the interference slope, $T _ { A A F , l }$ can be much higher than $T$ and the actual time extent of the baseband interference is determined by the repetition interval T . A similar effect appears if $t _ { R F I , l }$ is close to the ends of the repetition interval.

Using the previously introduced variables, it can be shown that the resulting analytical beat signal is written as:

$$
\begin{array} { r l r } {  { \boldsymbol { x } ( t ) = \{ \sum _ { i = 1 } ^ { N _ { T } } A _ { i } \mathrm { e x p } ( j 2 \pi k \tau _ { i } t ) +  } }  \\ & { } & { +  \sum _ { l = 1 } ^ { N _ { R F I } } A _ { R F I , l } \mathrm { e x p } [ j \pi ( k - k _ { R F I , l } ) ( t - t _ { R F I , l } ) ^ { 2 } ] \times  } \\ & { } & { \times  p ( \frac { t - t _ { R F I , l } } { T _ { A A F , l } } ) \} \times p ( \frac { t - \frac { T } { 2 } } { T } ) , } \end{array}\tag{6}
$$

where $A _ { i }$ is the complex amplitude of target i and A<sub>RF</sub> <sub>I,l</sub> is the complex amplitude of interference signal l. Hence, the beat signal is formed by a sum of constant frequency complex exponentials (corresponding to the targets) and a sum of baseband chirp signals that describe the interferences. Onwards, the sampled version of x(t) (with a sampling frequency $F _ { S } > 2 F _ { M } )$ is denoted with $x [ n ]$

## B. RFI mitigation method

This subsection presents the developed RFI mitigation algorithm based on time-frequency analysis. A general diagram of the algorithm is presented in Fig. 2 (the concept was developed by the authors within the PRYSTINE project [13]).

![](images/2b842e93a53cac26eaae4400307c05e907b7fcc1146d0db50d93f26c89e35375.jpg)  
Fig. 2: RFI mitigation method based on STFT and L-statistics.

The first step in the processing chain is to perform an STFT on the sampled beat signal $x [ n ]$ for each ramp. For a signal with a length of $N$ samples, the resulting STFT is:

$$
S T F T \{ x [ n ] \} [ m , k ] = \sum _ { n = 0 } ^ { N - 1 } x [ n ] w [ n - m N _ { s t e p } ] e ^ { - j 2 \pi n { \frac { k } { N } } } ,\tag{7}
$$

where $w [ n ]$ is the sliding window function (of size $N _ { w i n } )$ used to truncate the signal in shorter time intervals, m represents the frame number, k is the index of the frequency bin $( k =$ $0 , 1 , . . . , N { - } 1 )$ and $N _ { s t e p }$ is the step size of the sliding window. The total number of frames (the size of the STFT along the time axis) is denoted with $N _ { S T F T }$ . In order to have a smooth STFT along the time axis, a common practice is to have some overlap between succesive windows. The maximum value of the step size should not exceed half the size of the sliding window $( N _ { s t e p } < N _ { w i n } / 2 )$ and is usually chosen a fraction of $N _ { w i n } \ [ 1 4 ]$ . Using the introduced notations, it can be easily shown that the number of time bins of the STFT is:

$$
N _ { S T F T } = \left[ \frac { N - N _ { w i n } } { N _ { s t e p } } \right] + 1 .\tag{8}
$$

On the resulting spectrogram matrix (the squared absolute value of the STFT), the targets appear as constant frequency lines since they are complex sinusoidal signals (stationary components). The frequency concentration (width of the main lobe) and spectral resolution obtained by applying the STFT depends on the size of the window function used to truncate the beat signal.

For a beat signal with N samples (proportional with the duration of the transmitted chirp) and a sliding window with $N _ { w i n }$ samples, the main lobe for a point target will be approximatively $N / N _ { w i n }$ times wider than by performing a Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) on the whole signal (the spectral concentration is $T _ { c } / T _ { w }$ times lower). The spectral concentration could be restored by summing all the low concentrated complex STFT bins on each line k:

$$
S [ k ] = \sum _ { m = 0 } ^ { N _ { S T F T } - 1 } S T F T [ m , k ] .\tag{9}
$$

It can be shown [14] that the spectral concentration of $S [ k ]$ is close to the one obtained by directly applying the DFT on the whole beat signal. An example of spectrogram for two targets and one interference signal is shown in Fig. 4a. Notice that the uncorrelated interfering signal appears as a highly non-stationary component on the spectrogram, which is spread across multiple frequency bins.

The second step of the RFI mitigation algorithm is to perform a sorting of the spectrogram (in ascending order) over the time axis on each frequency line. A sample result is shown in Fig. 4b for the entire spectrogram. Since the beat signal corresponding to a real target is placed in the spectrogram at a constant frequency for all time instants, the sorting procedure will have a relatively low impact on the distribution of its values. However, this is not the case for the uncorrelated interference. In the initial spectrogram, the interference has high values only for a few time bins that are different from one frequency to another. After sorting the spectrogram, the interference has high values over its entire bandwidth, but only for a few time bins grouped together at the right side of the spectrogram.

Onwards, the range profile is computed by a coherent summation over the first $q \%$ of time bins on each frequency line of the sorted spectrogram. In data analysis, the approach of eliminating part of the data before analysing the rest, is usually known as L-statistics (estimation based on a linear combination of order statistics) [15]. In this case, the Lestimate of S[k] (denoted with $S _ { L } [ k ] )$ is

$$
S _ { L } ( k ) = \sum _ { m = 0 } ^ { N _ { Q } - 1 } \alpha _ { k } [ m ] ,\tag{10}
$$

where $\alpha _ { k } [ m ]$ represents the sorted STFT values of line k and $N _ { Q } = \left[ q \times N _ { S T F T } \right]$ is the number of bins that remain from the ordered spectrogram. By removing the largest values of the sorted spectrogram, we eliminate most of the bins affected by the interference. Therefore, after the partial summation we obtain a range profile containing the responses of the real targets with a noise floor level similar to the one without interference. Note that in the range profile obtained in this way we expect a spectral concentration $1 / q$ times lower than for the one obtained by summing all the available time bins (the lobes will be approximatively $1 / q$ wider).Additionally, we expect the SNR degradation to be below 1 dB for sumation percentages q around 0.9.

Fig. 3a shows a sample scenario with two targets and a high level of interference that raises the noise floor level such that the response of one target gets almost completely embedded in noise. After applying the RFI mitigation algorithm, the noise floor is restored to the original level and the two targets become clearly visible, but the level of the side-lobes is increased. This is caused by the fact that a continuous group of samples of the beat signal is eliminated when the interference is removed, which leads to an effect similar to an amplitude modulation of the beat signal.

The example shown in Fig. 3b emphasizes the impact of the RFI mitigation processing when no interference is present. In this case the effect on the side lobes of the range profile is reduced and the result is very similar to classical processing. This happens because the differences between the amplitudes of the bins containing a target’s response are mainly caused by noise (ideally they should be the same), and after the sorting operation the bins of a constant frequency line are shuffled more or less randomly (the removal of the bins with the highest amplitudes has a similar effect with randomly nulling samples from the initial beat signal, which does not have a strong impact on the final result if the number of removed samples is small). Hence, the method can be applied without an interference detection step because it has a very low impact on the range profile when no interference is present.

This idea of mitigating interference can be theoretically applied to eliminate any non-stationary signal from a spectrogram, and was originally employed in radar imaging analysis to remove micro-Doppler effects caused by fast moving parts [16]. However, this method cannot be applied for a correlated interference with the same ramp slope as the transmitted chirp since this type of interference will generate false stationary target signals.

## III. PARAMETRIC ANALYSIS OF THE RFI MITIGATION METHOD

From (10) we notice that the range profile obtained after RFI mitigation with the proposed method depends on the following parameters:

• sliding window’s length $N _ { w i n }$

• sliding window’s step size $N _ { s t e p } ,$

• summation percentage q.

For a single uncorrelated interference with linear chirp behaviour, the width of the interference pattern in the timefrequency plane is directly proportional to the sliding window’s length $N _ { w i n }$ , which gives the temporal resolution. In the case of several interference sources, the number of bins that have to be removed from the right hand side of the sorted spectrogram is roughly equal to $N _ { w i n } / N _ { s t e p }$ multipled with the number of interference sources plus an eventual guard interval (as qualitatively depicted for $N _ { s t e p } = 1$ in Fig. 4). In the following, the impact of the parameters $N _ { w i n } , N _ { s t e p }$ and $M _ { f }$ on the method’s performances is studied using the ARIM-v2 database (described in detail in [17]). This database comprises 24000 synthetic test signals that replicate automotive radar scenarios with variable number of targets and between 1 and 3 interference sources. The general parameters of the database are: 1.6 GHz bandwidth, 25.6 us chirp duration, 40 MHz sampling frequency (resulting 1024 beat signal samples) and 78 GHz central frequency.

The main performance metrics used to compare the results of different combinations of parameters are the mean absolute errors (MAE) of the target’s amplitudes and phases. The amplitude/phase MAE is computed as the difference between the estimated amplitudes/phases of the targets after RFI mitigation and the corresponding amplitudes/phases computed using the label signals without interference (the amplitude MAE is computed using the amplitude differences in decibels).

First, we evaluated the impact of the sliding window’s length $N _ { w i n }$ and the multiplication factor $M _ { f }$ for $N _ { s t e p } =$ 1 (the classical STFT definition) over the entire ARIMv2 dataset (containing signals affected by a variable number of interference sources). The results are synthesized in Table I. We observe that the best results are obtained when the multiplication factor is 3 or 4. Also, the smallest values of the amplitude/phase errors were obtained for $N _ { w i n } ~ = ~ 3 2$ and $N _ { w i n } = 6 4$ . As expected, the computation time is very similar in all cases because the step size is equal to one and the number of FFTs that have to be computed is similar for all window sizes. The computation time can be reduced by increaseing the step size, which leads to a lower number of FFTs. Hence, we analyzed the method’s performances when $N _ { s t e p }$ is larger than one for $N _ { w i n } = 3 2$ and 64, and $M _ { f } = 3$ and 4 (the values that led to the best results for $N _ { s t e p } = 1 )$ The results are also synthesized in Table I. With the increase of the step size we notice a slight increase of the amplitude and phase errors. Depending on the required computation time we may choose the corresponding step size by tolerating a certain performance degradation (the best computation time is obtained when the step size is half the sliding window’s length).

## IV. COMPARISON WITH STATE OF THE ART

In this section we compare the proposed RFI mitigation approach based on STFT and L-statistics with a classical “detect and omit” (or ,,zeroing”) technique [4], [18] (in which the beat signal samples that are affected by interference are nulled) and a state-of-the-art method that uses a fully convolutional network [17], [19]. The comparison was based on the ARIM-v2 dataset and real data acquired with the NXP TEF810X 77 GHz radar transceiver.

In Table II we present a comparison between the methods in terms of amplitude/phase MAE on the ARIM-v2 dataset (the values for the “zeroing” technique and the FCN-based method are taken directly from [17] since the same dataset and performance metrics are used). We note that the proposed method provides the best results for optimal combinations of parameters identified in Section III.

A more detailed analysis was performed with a couple of signals of the ARIM-v2 dataset to compare the proposed method with the “zeroing” technique. The performances of the two methods are compared for various durations of the interference relative to the beat signal length. The interference duration depends on the difference between the slope of the transmitted chirp and the slope of the interference (see (5)). In the case of a continuous wave interference, the slope of the interference in the beat signal domain is the same as the slope of the transmitted signal, and the interference duration is short relative to the repetition interval (the interference is well localized within the repetition interval). However, in the case of a chirp-type interference with a slope comparable to the one of the transmitted signal, the slope of the interference appearing in the beat signal gets smaller, and the duration of the interference can cover a relatively large part of the beat signal. This effect is shown in Fig. 5. For a short duration RFI, the two methods provide similar results, and the nulling technique has the advantage of a much simpler implementation. In the case of a long duration RFI, when the nulling mask is applied on the beat signal, the useful signal’s energy is also diminished and the target is hardly distinguishable from the noise floor. In this particular situation the proposed method clearly provides better results.

## V. CONCLUSION

In this paper we presented a novel RFI mitigation method that employs an L-statistics approach on the beat signal’s short-time Fourier transform to eliminate non-coherent interference signals. A parametric analysis of the method was performed on a synthetic dataset to determine the combination of parameters that provide the best results in terms of amplitude/phase MAE and computation time. The proposed method is able to surpass the performance of classical and recent works on RFI mitigation, does not require an interference detection step and also works for signals affected by multiple interference sources. Future work aims a real-time implementaton of the STFT and L-statistic method on a digital signal processor.
