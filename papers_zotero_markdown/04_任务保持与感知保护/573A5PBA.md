# Real-Time Interference Mitigation for Reliable Target Detection with FMCW Radar in Interference Environments

Youlong Weng <sup>1,2,†</sup> , Ziang Zhang <sup>1,2,†</sup> , Guangzhi Chen <sup>1,2,</sup>\* , Yaru Zhang <sup>1</sup>, Jiabao Chen <sup>1</sup> and Hongzhan Song <sup>1</sup>

Abstract: Frequency-modulated continuous-wave (FMCW) millimeter-wave (mmWave) radar systems are increasingly utilized in environmental sensing due to their high range resolution and robust sensing ability in severe weather environments. However, mutual interference among radar systems significantly degrades the target detection capability. Recent advancements in interference mitigation utilizing deep learning (DL) approaches have demonstrated promising results. DL-based approaches typically have high computational costs, which makes them unsuitable for real-time applications with strict latency requirements and limited computing resources. In this paper, we propose an efficient solution for real-time radar interference mitigation. A lightweight transformer, which is smaller and faster than the baseline transformer, is designed to reduce interference. The integration of linear attention mechanisms with depthwise separable convolutions significantly reduces the network’s computational complexity while maintaining a comparable performance. In addition, a two-stage knowledge distillation (KD) process is deployed to compress the network and enhance its efficiency. The staged distillation approach alleviates the training difficulties associated with substantial differences between the teacher and student networks. Both simulated and real-world experiments demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms the state-of-the-art methods while achieving high processing speeds.

## 1. Introduction

A radar is a robust sensor used in environmental sensing applications, such as autonomous driving and remote sensing, which enables target detection even under adverse weather conditions [1,2]. Frequency-modulated continuous-wave (FMCW) millimeterwave (mmWave) radar systems offer a higher resolution and have lower manufacturing costs than conventional radars [3–5]. However, the mass deployment of FMCW radar systems in congested areas has led to escalating concerns regarding interference between the radar systems [6]. The limited spectrum poses serious challenges for reliable target detection in interference environments.

An adaptive noise canceller (ANC) has been used to reduce interference in the frequency domain through the least mean squares algorithm [7]. In [8], wavelet denoising (WD) was applied to extract the interference signal. A constant false alarm rate detector (CFAR)-based framework was proposed in [9] to mitigate interference for FMCW radars. In the framework, interference was detected and eliminated in the time-frequency domain. Wang et al. [10] presented a Matrix Pencil (MP)-based interference suppression method, exploiting the fact that the desired beat signals were the sum of exponential sinusoidal components and the interferences were the sum of chirp-like waveforms. In [11], the received radar signal was partitioned into sub-bands, and then the interference was mitigated in each sub-band. In addition, several works have investigated signal decomposition-based approaches. In [12], the interference signal was decomposed into a set of components via empirical mode decomposition (EMD), and the interference-dominated components were determined by the consecutive mean squares error (CMSE) algorithm. Variational mode decomposition (VMD) was also used to decompose the received radar signal and to separate the target signals from the interference [13]. Subsequently, an interference suppression method based on outlier detection and VMD was proposed [14]. In [15], a sparsity-based technique was presented for radar interference suppression, and the alternating direction method of the multiplier-based technique was applied to restore the radar echoes. An interference mitigation optimization problem was designed in [16] to extract the target echoes with a row-sparse constraint by taking advantage of the sparsity of the interference signal in the time domain and the row sparsity of the useful echo signal in the frequency domain. In [17], a bi-level-based signal optimization model was derived by using L1-norm penalized least squares to reduce interference.

Recently, the effectiveness of DL-based interference suppression methods has been demonstrated. Recurrent neural networks (RNNs) have been used to deal with interference in the time domain [18,19]. In [20], the interference was mitigated by using a generative adversarial network (GAN). Another type of strategy was to convert the interfered signal into the time-frequency domain and perform two-dimensional processing. In [21], a complex-valued fully convolutional network (CV-FCN)-based architecture effectively suppressed the interference by exploiting prior features as a regularization term. Then, to further improve the interference mitigation performance, dilated convolution and designated contrast learning strategy were utilized for network construction and training, respectively [22]. By integrating the data-driven properties of DL and the advantages of compressed sensing (CS)-based interpretable optimization, an efficient feature-oriented unsupervised adaptive network called FUAS-Net was presented to suppress mutual interference [23]. The transformer is a powerful and promising technology in signal processing [24]. Transformer-based networks are especially adept at capturing repetitive patterns with extended dependencies, which are well suited for reconstructing the periodic target signals from the interfered signal. In preliminary work [25], the authors developed a novel end-to-end transformer-based interference mitigation structure named RIMformer, which performs well in interference cancellation and target signal reconstruction.

However, most DL-based approaches have a common drawback. The architectures of deep neural networks tend to be deeper and wider in order to obtain better results, which places high demands on the computational and memory costs for inference. The high memory and computational costs hinder the deployment of deep neural networks on resource-constrained devices. The deployment of DL models on edge computing systems poses a significant burden. Real interference mitigation tasks require a balance between performance and processing speed. The lightweight design of models is necessary to achieve decent resource requirements and retain a high interference mitigation performance. Model compression and acceleration of deep neural networks are needed to satisfy the realtime constraints of real-world tasks. Lightweight networks such as MobileNet [26] and ShuffleNet [27] have been developed for convolutional neural networks (CNNs). For transformer networks, reducing the complexity of attention computations is a straightforward approach to minimizing computing cost while maintaining high recognition accuracy [28]. To tackle the extensive computation demands in self-attention layers, long-short-range attention (LSRA) [29] was introduced to extract global and local features separately. The Reformer [30] adopts locality-sensitive hashing to substitute dot product operations in attention, which reduces the computation requirements. The Linformer [31] decomposes self-attention into multiple low-rank matrices via linear projection, and the complexity is decreased from $O ( n ^ { 2 } )$ to $O ( n )$ . Knowledge distillation (KD) is also an effective model compression technique aimed at transferring knowledge from a more extensive network to a smaller one [32]. KD algorithms are further improved through alternative loss functions like contrastive-based loss [33] and minimizing the maximum mean discrepancy between models [34]. A direct distillation approach [35] was proposed to leverage the teacher’s classifier and align intermediate features with an L2 loss. In [36], a teacher-free KD employed self-training and manually designed regularization terms to improve the student model.

On the basis of our preliminary work [25], we introduce a lightweight transformer based on linear attention and depthwise separable convolution to mitigate interference and restore target signals. In order to address the performance loss caused by network size reduction, a KD strategy is applied by combining both feature-based and response-based knowledge, which can significantly improve the performance of the student network. It is observed that the effectiveness of KD decreases dramatically when there is a substantial disparity in complexity between the teacher and student models. To overcome this drawback, a medium-sized assistant model is utilized to bridge the gap between the teacher and student models, which facilitates more effective knowledge transfer.

In [25], we introduced a transformer-based framework named RIMformer to mitigate interferences in FMCW mmWave radar systems. The proposed RIMformer is designed with an end-to-end architecture, which can deal with the interfered radar signals in the time domain. As depicted in Figure 3, the network adopts a typical encoder–decoder structure and comprises the following five main parts: input segmentation; channel expansion; feature extraction; signal reconstruction; and the merging of reconstructed signal frames. The proposed RIMformer block acts as an encoder and decoder at different stages. The encoder extracts features from the intermediate frequency signals during the feature extraction phase. The decoder reconstructs the target signal using key feature maps in the signal reconstruction phase. A distinctive aspect of the RIMformer block structure is its use of dual-path self-attention calculations combined with convolutions for local feature extraction, which improves the performance of the network in terms of capturing the features of the beneficial components of the interfered signals at different scales.

The structure of the RIMformer block is shown in Figure 4. The IF signal of the radar system is first transformed by a sliding window algorithm into a structured format consisting of the frame number, the frame length, and the channel. Each frame represents a localized portion of the signal and preserves temporal continuity through the overlap. This operation transforms the 1D signal into structured 2D data, allowing for the localized analysis of the adjacent frame. After the sliding window operation, the reassembled data block is then expanded along the channel dimension using a 1D convolution layer. This increases the feature richness by projecting each frame into a higher-dimensional feature space. These segmented signals are analyzed along the frame number and length, with attention mechanisms computed within each frame (intra-frame) and across the frames (inter-frame). Intra-frame attention captures short-term signal correlations, whereas inter-frame attention reflects long-term dependencies across the sequence. The normalized attention results are combined with the original input via residual connections, forming the final output of the attention layer. The next layer is a fully connected feedforward network (FFN), including a hidden layer and an activation function for non-linear transformation of the features at each position. The output from the FFN is subjected to layer normalization and residual connections to ensure data stability. The introduction of the convolutional block enables the use of CNNs for local feature extraction. Each block comprises two convolutional layers, a linear layer, and several activation functions. The gated linear unit (GLU) [37] is positioned between the two convolutional layers. The GLU dynamically adjusts its activation level and output in response to the input signal’s strength and characteristics, providing enhanced adaptability and flexibility for processing complex input data. The commonly used mean squared error (MSE) loss function is limited to capturing discrepancies among predicted signals solely in the time domain. Therefore, in the RIMformer, a hybrid time-frequency loss function is designed to encompass the errors of the reconstructed signal in both the temporal and frequency domains. The hybrid loss function is calculated as follows:

## 2.2. Linear Attention Mechanism

The RIMformer architecture employs a trainable dual attention mechanism to discern complex dependencies between elements of the input sequence, which is crucial for capturing intricate relational information. However, the computational complexity of conventional transformers scales quadratically with the number of signal frames n in the input sequence. This quadratic scaling becomes impractical for an enormous n. The quadratic computational complexity makes the straightforward use of global receptive fields in self-attention intractable, which leads to excessively high computational costs.

While this problem can be addressed by designing sparse global attention patterns or using smaller attention windows, it is often limited by the intricacies of the attention patterns designed and may compromise the ability to model long-term dependencies. In contrast, linear attention is an efficacious alternative and reduces the computational complexity from $O ( n ^ { 2 } )$ to $O ( n )$ . In the paper, a method for estimating the softmax and Gaussian kernels using positive orthogonal random features is applied to the RIMformer, employing FAVOR+ [31] for the robust and unbiased estimation of standard (softmax) attention. Specifically, a kernel function is introduced to approximate the original similarity function, which can enhance computational efficiency while preserving the accuracy of the attention mechanism. The standard scaled-dot attention can be described as follows:

$$
\mathrm { A t t e n t i o n } ( Q , K , V ) = \mathrm { s o f t m a x } \left( \frac { Q K ^ { T } } { \sqrt { d } } \right) V ,\tag{3}
$$

where the inputs Q, K, $V \in \mathbb { R } ^ { n \times d }$ are queries, keys, and values derived from the input by different linear transformations. d is the number of feature dimension of signal frames. Radar signals, after applying the sliding window, are transformed into a structured format with overlapping segments. These segments capture the localized temporal and spatial features of the signals, including interference and target echoes. The query Q and key K metrices represent the characteristics of these segments. Their dot product measures the similarity between different signal segments, which is the key to identifying interference patterns and correlations in the radar data. ## 2.3. Depthwise Separable Convolution

In addition to the self-attention computation, the convolution operation has the largest computational overhead in the RIMformer block. Radar signals after the sliding window are represented as multi-channel data with localized temporal and spatial features. Convolutions efficiently extract and integrate these features. In a regular convolution operation, the input feature map of shape $N \times M \times C _ { i n }$ is convolved with a filter tensor of shape $K \times K \times C _ { i n } \times C _ { o u t } ,$ where N is the number of signal frames, M is the length of each frame, and $C _ { i n }$ is the number of input channels. The filter size is $K \times K ,$ and the number of output channels is $C _ { o u t }$ . The number of operations required for one output feature map is as follows:

$$
C _ { r e g } = K \times K \times C _ { i n } \times C _ { o u t } \times N \times M .\tag{7}
$$

Depthwise separable convolution [26] breaks down a standard convolution into a depthwise convolution, followed by $\mathsf { a } \mathrm { ~ 1 ~ \times ~ 1 ~ }$ convolution called a pointwise convolution. Standard convolution filters the inputs and combines them into a new set of outputs in one step. Depthwise separable convolution splits this step into two layers, one for filtering and the other for combining. Specifically, depthwise convolution applies a single filter per input channel. Pointwise convolution is then used to create linear combinations of the outputs of the depth layer. Significant reductions in computational workload and model size can be achieved by representing convolution as a two-step process of filtering and combining.

In depthwise convolution, each input channel is convolved with its own set of filters independently and the computational complexity is as follows:

$$
C _ { d e p t h } = K \times K \times C _ { i n } \times N \times M .\tag{8}
$$

The computational complexity for pointwise convolution is as follows:

$$
C _ { p o i n t } = 1 \times 1 \times C _ { i n } \times C _ { o u t } \times N \times M .\tag{9}
$$

Therefore, the ratio of the computational complexity of depthwise separable convolution and regular convolution is as follows:

$$
\begin{array} { c l } { { \displaystyle \frac { C _ { d e p t h } + C _ { p o i n t } } { C _ { r e g } } = \frac { \left( K \times K + C _ { o u t } \right) \times \left( C _ { i n } \times N \times M \right) } { K \times K \times C _ { i n } \times C _ { o u t } \times N \times M } } } \\ { { \displaystyle = \frac { 1 } { K ^ { 2 } } + \frac { 1 } { C _ { o u t } } . } } \end{array}\tag{10}
$$

It can be concluded that for the RIMformer with $K = 3$ , the use of depthwise separable convolution requires less than $\frac 1 8$ of the computational complexity of regular convolution.

## 2.4. Two-Stage KD

It has been observed that when the discrepancy between the teacher and student networks is too high, there is a decline in the efficiency of KD. To address this challenge, we propose a multi-step KD approach involving an intermediate-sized network (an assistant model) to bridge the gap between the student and teacher networks. The overall process is depicted in Figure 5. By introducing an intermediate assistant teacher model, we create a smoother knowledge transfer process. The intermediate assistant teacher model incorporates lightweight design improvements but retains a structure closer to the original teacher. It acts as a “bridge” by effectively distilling the critical features and representations from the teacher model in a simplified form. The student model can then learn from the assistant model in the second stage, with improved alignment due to the significantly reduced gap in model capacity and complexity. The two-stage approach strikes a balance between computational efficiency and performance. Although the addition of an intermediate model introduces an additional training step, this step is essential to help the lightweight student model achieve competitive performance in interference mitigation without sacrificing the benefits of the proposed lightweight designs.

The distillation process integrates response-based knowledge and feature-based knowledge. Response-based knowledge refers to the neural responses of the teacher model’s final output layer. The principal idea is to have the student model directly emulate the teacher model’s final predictions. Data representations from the intermediate layers can also serve as knowledge for training the student model, also known as feature-based knowledge. In this paper, the L2 norm is utilized to measure the similarity between the feature maps and the output layer. The distillation stage loss function is represented as follows:

$$
\mathcal { L } _ { \mathrm { K D } } = \frac { 1 } { 2 } \Big [ L _ { \mathrm { R M S E } } \Big ( K _ { \mathrm { F } } , ~ \widetilde { K } _ { \mathrm { F } } \Big ) + L _ { \mathrm { R M S E } } \Big ( K _ { \mathrm { R } } , ~ \widetilde { K } _ { \mathrm { R } } \Big ) \Big ] .\tag{11}
$$

![](images/a280b762a0ad00f60acc510767fc7f4f2f56df972c68f007b9b3633cadda4b57.jpg)  
Figure 5. Architecture diagram for two-stage KD.

The overall training loss function is a weighted sum of the distillation loss and the actual value of the time-frequency mixed loss, which can be calculated as follows:

$$
\mathcal { L } = \alpha \times \mathcal { L } _ { \mathrm { K D } } + \left( 1 - \alpha \right) \times \mathcal { L } _ { \mathrm { T F } } ,\tag{12}
$$

where α is a weight that balances the two parts of the loss.

Furthermore, KD is optimized through dynamic loss balancing. As shown in Figure 6, the proposed training strategy involves adjusting the weight parameter in the network loss function throughout the training process. Initially, the integration of distilled knowledge from the teacher network is accentuated with a large weight, which fosters a swift convergence of the student network towards an optimal solution. Subsequently, a medium weight strikes a balance between the influences of KD and label prediction losses. The student network’s comprehension is refined with mitigated excessive reliance on pre-existing knowledge. Lastly, the employment of a smaller weight in later stages alleviates the risk of overfitting the teacher’s knowledge and facilitates generalization to unseen data. This dynamic methodology provides a nuanced and efficient approach to distill knowledge while ensuring the adaptability and resilience of the student network.

The primary metrics used for the evaluation of interference mitigation performance are the signal-to-interference plus noise ratio (SINR) and the peak signal-to-interference plus noise ratio (PSINR). The SINR measures the power ratio of the reconstructed target signal to the sum of interference and noise, which provides a metric to evaluate the quality of the reconstructed target signal in the presence of interference and noise. The SINR can be calculated as follows:

$$
\mathrm { S I N R } = 1 0 \log _ { 1 0 } \left( \frac { s ^ { 2 } } { ( s - \hat { s } ) ^ { 2 } } \right) ,\tag{13}
$$

where s and sˆ are the reference signal and the reconstructed target, respectively.

The PSINR is defined as the ratio of the mean power across the target peaks and the mean power of interference and noise in the frequency domain, which can be calculated as follows:

$$
\begin{array} { r } { \mathrm { P S I N R } = 1 0 \log _ { 1 0 } \left( \frac { \frac { 1 } { \# _ { \mathcal { T } } } \sum _ { i \in \mathcal { T } } | s [ i ] | ^ { 2 } } { \frac { 1 } { \# _ { \mathcal { N } } } \sum _ { i \in \mathcal { N } } | s [ i ] | ^ { 2 } } \right) , } \end{array}\tag{14}
$$

where i is the index of the signals s; T is the set of target peaks; N is the set of background noise and noise caused by interference; and # denotes the cardinality of a set.

For the fairness of the experiments, the same hyperparameters are used for all training processes. Specifically, models are trained using the Adam optimizer, with 600 epochs of cosine learning rate decay and 50 epochs of linear warm-up. The base learning rate is set to $1 \times 1 0 ^ { - 4 }$ and the batch size is 32. Additionally, a KD weight of 0.5 is employed to calculate the final loss. Both interference mitigation performance and inference latency are tested across multiple hardware platforms, including a server CPU (AMD EPYC 7F72), a GPU (Nvidia RTX 4090), and a low-power edge computing platform (Jetson Orin NX 8 GB).

The efficacy of the introduced linear attention mechanism and depthwise separable convolutions is assessed for the lightweight design structure. The linear attention mechanism is implemented to replace the traditional attention mechanism, reducing the computational load from quadratic to linear complexity with respect to the sequence length. Depthwise separable convolutions are used to reduce the number of parameters and the computational cost of convolutional layers, with a slight reduction in the model’s ability to capture spatial features. The network with the linear attention mechanism and deep separable convolution has a significantly reduced number of parameters and inference time compared to the baseline RIMformer while maintaining its performance.

The original RIMformer consisted of 362.99 M FLOPs, with an SINR of 11.68 dB, a PSINR of 21.83 dB, and an inference time on the Jetson platform of 177.24 ms. After introducing the linear attention mechanism, the FLOPs are reduced by approximately 48. 8%, and the inference time is decreased by 40.7%. However, the PSINR remains at 21.50 dB. In addition, the impact of the linear attention mechanism on memory overhead is significant, as it saves more than 1 GB of memory space. The depthwise separable convolution leads to a 12.6% reduction in FLOPs, with PSINR remaining stable at 21.31 dB. The combined application of both techniques results in a 61.4% reduction in parameters and a 45.7% reduction in inference time, with the PSINR improving to 21.41 dB. These results show that the linear attention mechanism and the depthwise separable convolution dramatically reduce the computational complexity of the network and decrease the computation time.

The decrease in the number of network layers also leads to a reduction in complexity and inference time but with a slight decrease in PSINR. The lightest network is 5.3 times faster at inference in the Jetson platform compared to the baseline model. For models with two layers, the use of a lightweight design is equally helpful in reducing complexity. In the six-layer models, the lightweight design module has less impact on the results. This is due to the redundancy of deep networks. Simply reducing the number of layers in the network results in a decrease in interference suppression, as demonstrated in Table 2. Therefore, there is a need for KD, which allows for the knowledge learned in the deep network to migrate into the shallow network.

It can be observed that the changes in inference time varies considerably across the different platforms. Specifically, the lightweight design demonstrates favorable outcomes on the CPU and Jetson platforms but exhibits an increased processing time on the GPU. This discrepancy occurs because the lightweight models fail to fully leverage the computational capabilities of the GPU. Lightweight optimizations have proven to be particularly advantageous for edge computing devices.

The ablation studies demonstrate that the incorporation of a linear attention mechanism and a deep separable convolution significantly reduces the network size and inference time while slightly reducing the interference mitigation performance. These improvements make the network more efficient and more suitable for real-time applications.

## 3.3. The Effect of Two-Stage KD

A two-stage KD process is employed to further reduce the complexity of the network. An intermediate model with six layers of lightweight RIMformer blocks is trained first, which serves as a bridge between the large teacher model and the final student model. The assistant teacher model is then used to train a smaller, more efficient student model with two layers of lightweight RIMformer blocks. The effectiveness of two-stage KD is analyzed by comparing direct training, one-stage KD, and two-stage KD. In direct training, the student model is trained directly on the dataset. While in one-stage KD, the student model is trained using the teacher model, and the loss weight α is set to 0.5 in the intermediate process of dynamic distillation.

Figure 8 shows the results for the models with different training methods, including the PSINR and SINR. The performance of the model using one-stage KD is inferior to that of the directly trained model. This is because the significant differences between the teacher and student models hinder the effective transfer of knowledge from the former to the latter during the training phase. Two-stage distillation results in a smaller student model with an improvement in both the PSINR and SINR compared to direct training and one-stage KD.

A corner reflector serves as a target at a distance of approximately 10 m from the victim radar. The victim radar also receives the signals reflected from the two interfering radar boards, therefore the two interfering radar boards behave as two stationary targets. The specific radar parameter settings are presented in Table 4. For the victim radar, the initial frequency is set at 77.0 GHz. ## 4. Discussion

The proposed method consistently outperforms the other three methods at different input SNRs. When the input SNR is a relatively high value, the proposed lightweight

RIMformer exhibits excellent interference suppression performance. However, when the SNR is extremely low, the correlations among the distinct distance elements of IF signal becomes weak, and the network captures limited interference features. Therefore, the interference cannot be completely suppressed.

Robustness Under Various Radar and Interference Conditions: The results of the measured experiments indicate that the lightweight model effectively translates from simulated to real-world scenarios and achieves good performance metrics. To further validate the robustness of the lightweight model, the dataset is reconstructed to cover a wider range of radar parameter configurations and more challenging interference conditions. Compared to the parameter configurations in Section 3, the start frequency, duration, and slope of the victim radar are adjusted from single fixed values of 77 GHz, 20 µs, and 30 $\mathrm { M H z } / \mu \mathrm { s }$ to commonly used ranges of (76.5 GHz, 77.5 GHz), (10 µs, 40 µs), and (10 MHz/µs, 60 MHz/µs), respectively. Additionally, the number of interfering radars is extended from a range of (3, 7) to (1, 10). The lightweight RIMformer and other methods are applied to suppress interference in the reconstructed dataset. Potential Limitations: Although the proposed lightweight design and two-stage KD in the RIMformer network offer substantial improvements in model size and inference time, several avenues remain open for future research, including the following: (1) In extremely low SNR conditions, the interference mitigation performance of the network is less pronounced. This suggests that the network might struggle with input signals that are heavily corrupted by noise and require additional pre-processing steps to enhance the performance further; (2) There is an inherent trade-off between model complexity and performance. Future work can explore the optimal balance based on the proposed approach.

## 5. Conclusions

The objective of this paper is to address the issue of real-time interference mitigation for reliable target detection with FMCW radar in interference environments. The RIMformer network is improved to be lightweight by integrating the linear attention mechanism and the depthwise separable convolution, which reduce computational complexity and network size while maintaining interference mitigation performance. Additionally, we implement a two-stage KD process to further optimize the network by training an intermediate-sized assistant teacher model before training the final student model. Compared to the original RIMformer, the improved framework significantly reduces the network size and inference time with a slight SINR loss, achieving a good balance between performance and processing delay. Both simulations and experiments demonstrate the promising performance and inference time of the lightweight RIMformer. The proposed framework provides an efficient solution for real-time FMCW radar applications with strict latency requirements and limited computing resources.
